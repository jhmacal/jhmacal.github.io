AI

Designing the next decade of legal practice

The next decade of legal practice will not be defined by AI tools. It will be defined by the practitioners who design how AI is applied within the law.

Most attorneys will be users. They will learn the products, deploy them in client work, and treat AI as a productivity layer that lives outside their actual reasoning. A small number will be designers. They will encode legal reasoning into the systems themselves, define what those systems should consider authoritative, and shape how AI engages with regulatory law, agency precedent, and the actual work of running an immigration program.

The distinction is not academic. Users and designers operate at different points in the value chain. Users absorb tools. Designers shape what tools become.

I sit in the second group.

I have been integrating AI into legal practice since 2021. I build custom research pipelines, agentic case-strategy systems, and program-reporting automation that operates inside my actual workload, not adjacent to it. I design AI reasoning environments for federal administrative law, encoding attorney-grade reasoning into structures that machines can evaluate against ground truth.

This work is technical. It is also deeply legal. The two are not separable when you are designing the systems themselves. A practitioner who has not done both cannot do either correctly.

What this means in practice is that the immigration programs I run produce outputs faster, with better visibility, at higher confidence, and with workflows that scale with the business. The AI is not a tool I reach for at the end of a process. It is part of the architecture I have built into the program.

The firms that will lead the next decade of legal hiring will be the ones that recognize this distinction early. They will hire designers, not users. They will build immigration programs and broader legal functions on architecture that anticipates what AI is becoming, not what it was.

I am a designer.

The shape of legal practice ten years from now is already visible.

Immigration as a function will not survive in its current form. The cost structure, the risk surface, the pace of regulatory change, and the need for cross-functional coordination across HR, Talent, Finance, and Tax are all moving in the same direction. The model where outside counsel files what HR coordinators describe, with no one inside the company owning the decision architecture, is a model with an expiration date.

What replaces it is an embedded function with attorney-grade leadership and AI-native operating infrastructure. The candidate who builds that function will be the candidate who has done the work to understand both halves: the legal substance and the systems that operationalize it.

I have spent the last five years preparing for that role. I built a practice that runs at scale. I designed program structures that coordinate across functions. I integrated AI into the workflow before the legal market did. I write about the structural problem because solving it is what I do.

The work is ready. The role is what comes next.
