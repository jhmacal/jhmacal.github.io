⚠️

The H-1B Cap Problem Is Structural — Not Temporary

At 35.3% selection rate (FY2026), approximately 65% of cap-subject registrations are rejected by lottery. For every 10 international engineers Legora tries to hire who need cap-subject H-1B, 6–7 would not be selected. Without alternative pathways built into the recruitment process from day one, Legora would systematically lose the majority of its international candidate pipeline to lottery risk.

Starting in FY2027, the lottery is not purely random. DOL classifies H-1B prevailing wages at four levels. When USCIS implemented wage-preference selection, Levels III and IV — which represent a smaller share of total cap-subject registrations — were selected at materially higher rates than Levels I and II.

What determines wage level? SOC code selection. The same role — a senior engineer, a product attorney, a legal technologist — can be mapped to different Standard Occupational Classification codes, each with its own prevailing wage floor and corresponding wage level. The occupational classification choice made at LCA stage sets the wage level that applies to the petition.

In FY2027, a global mobility function would audit every role before registration: match it to the optimal SOC code, position wage levels at III or IV where the role genuinely supports it, and file deliberately rather than by default. This would improve petition defensibility, reduce RFE exposure, and — because higher-wage registrations now have better lottery odds — meaningfully improve selection outcomes for Legora's cap-subject pipeline. This is not a legal workaround. It is program design.

The program is always larger than the filing record: O-1A petitions carry no labor market test. OPT and STEM OPT authorizations require no sponsorship filing. EB-1A and EB-2 NIW bypass labor certification entirely. Cap-exempt transfers in certain circumstances require no new filing if wages are unchanged. A well-designed global mobility program at Legora will be significantly larger than what any external analysis can see -- which is also why competitor benchmarking systematically understates actual program sophistication at companies like Ironclad.

At 300 employees with a significant international engineering and legal hiring profile, the $1.5M–$3M reactive figure is not a hypothetical worst case. It is the arithmetic of 68–140 nonimmigrant matters billed at BigLaw rates, 11–29 immigrant visa cycles, and premium processing applied reflexively across the portfolio rather than strategically. The comparison understates the true gap. Premium processing decisions made deliberately — filing standard and upgrading only when a case approaches a critical deadline — produce material annual savings at Legora's filing volume. Stronger initial petitions reduce RFE rates, and each avoided RFE saves $2,000–$5,000 in outside counsel response costs and weeks of processing delay. An in-house function structured around peer-level outside counsel management — competitive reviews, billing accountability, and negotiated rates — generates 8 to 15 percent in rate reductions on annual outside counsel spend. The advisory retainer, typically $50,000–$150,000 per year at comparable volume, is absorbed internally at no marginal cost. An in-house function does not simply replace the reactive spend. It changes the quality and cost of everything the outside spend produces.

The financial case is compelling on the numbers. It is stronger on everything that comes before them. The cost of building the function early is fixed and bounded. The cost of not building it is not. A single senior hire lost to an H-1B lottery that had an available cap-exempt pathway, a PERM audit triggered by incomplete recruitment documentation, an India-born engineer at year four who discovers the EB-2 priority date is July 2014 and leaves for a role with a faster route — any one of these produces a cost that exceeds the function's annual budget. These are not edge cases. They are the predictable consequences of scaling international hiring without the infrastructure to manage it. Legora is at exactly the stage where building this early costs the least and produces the most. Every month of delay is a month of reactive exposure, compounding.

The Infrastructure Behind the 300 Hires

The Series D was raised to "fuel North American growth." That phrase has a precise operational meaning: targeting 300+ employees across the region. A meaningful share of that talent pool requires active global mobility coordination to hire, onboard, and retain on that timeline.

Legora is hiring its Global Mobility Manager. That may reflect where the program currently sits in its build. What the peer data makes clear is that the inflection point to establish this function -- before international hiring velocity makes the infrastructure question urgent -- is now. The next 90 days determine whether Legora enters the hiring surge with infrastructure in place or assembles it under pressure.

1

Pathway Analysis at the Moment of Offer

Every international candidate receives a pathway assessment at the moment of offer — not after. The question is never "can we sponsor this person" but "which pathway, on what timeline, with what certainty." Playbooks codifies the eligibility logic — visa category routing by role, SOC code, and country of birth — so the assessment happens in days, not weeks. Portal gives candidates and internal teams real-time visibility into where their matter stands.

2

Cap-Exempt Transfers from International Offices

Employees at international offices targeted for US roles have cap-exempt pathways available now. Executives and senior leaders qualify for intracompany transferee status in a managerial or executive capacity — a direct path to permanent residency without labor certification. Engineers and specialized staff with 12 or more continuous months at international offices within the preceding 3 years qualify for the specialized knowledge pathway. Neither depends on the lottery. Workflows automates the filing cascade. Tabular Review manages the documentation audit trail at scale.

3

Treaty-Based Recruiting Tracks

For Australian nationals — a meaningful segment of the legal engineering talent pool — consular processing offers the fastest turnaround of any work authorization category. This pathway is chronically underused in legal tech relative to the available talent pool. The same is true for Singapore nationals under a separate treaty pathway.

4

Lottery-Independent Parallel Tracks

A recruiting plan that relies on the lottery as the primary pathway for international hires will fail at scale. Every cap-subject candidate is assessed simultaneously for cap-exempt alternatives, OPT and STEM extension eligibility, and treaty-based pathways. The FY2028 lottery opens in March 2027 — building the right candidates into the right tracks now is what determines whether the 300-hire plan succeeds on schedule or gets compressed by immigration lag. Legal Research surfaces current priority date movement in real time. Workflows manages the multi-track pipeline without manual coordination overhead.

5

Green Card Clock Started Early

The data on comparable companies at this stage is consistent: the companies that initiated labor certification in year two or three retained their sponsored employees through the process. The ones that waited until year four or five discovered the conversation had to happen retroactively — after employees had already begun evaluating alternatives.

6

Extraordinary Ability and National Interest Waiver

For employees born in India or China, the standard employer-sponsored green card track carries a backlog currently measured in decades under the EB-2 category as of the May 2026 Visa Bulletin. The employer-sponsored extraordinary ability pathway and the national interest waiver are the only routes to permanent residency on a timeline that makes retention realistic. Both bypass labor certification entirely. For senior AI researchers and published engineers, the qualifying record often already exists.

  • Australian talent recruited through E-3 pipeline
  • PERM initiated for earliest-eligible employees

  • I-9 compliance framework established ahead of scale
